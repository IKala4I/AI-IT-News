### Mythos 5 Partially Unblocked — But Fable 5 Stays Frozen

After 16 days of a complete access blackout, the US government partially lifted its block on Anthropic's Mythos 5 model on June 26. Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick wrote that "appropriate safeguards are in place to permit certain trusted partners to access the Claude Mythos 5 Model," clearing roughly 100 vetted US companies and federal agencies to use it. Fable 5 — Anthropic's stronger general-purpose model — remains fully restricted with no timeline given. Anthropic has separately stated that Mythos 5, its most capable cybersecurity-focused model, will now be available specifically to cyber defenders and critical infrastructure providers in this limited cohort.

**Why it matters:** This is a partial resolution of the standoff covered last week, but it's a careful half-step, not a reopening. The distinction between Mythos 5 (allowed to a curated list) and Fable 5 (still frozen) suggests the government's concern is specifically with the most capable general reasoning model, not the cybersecurity specialist. For the ~100 approved partners, access resumes; for everyone else — including Anthropic's paying customers worldwide — the situation is unchanged. The lack of any general restoration date keeps the policy uncertainty alive and the international access question entirely unresolved.

### GPT-5.6 Officially Launches — Behind a Government Gate

OpenAI did ship GPT-5.6 on June 26 — but the Trump administration required it to go out only to approximately 20 hand-picked "trusted partners," not to the general public. The release comprises three models: **Sol** (flagship, $5/$30 per million input/output tokens), **Terra** (balanced everyday use, ~half Sol's price), and **Luna** (fast and affordable, $1/$6 per million tokens). OpenAI says broader ChatGPT, Codex, and API access is coming "in the coming weeks," once government review expands the approved list. The delayed launch itself is notable: last week's digests tracked Polymarket odds collapsing from 83% to 18% on a June window — the model arrived at the wire, but gated.

**Why it matters:** GPT-5.6 Sol carries a redesigned reward audit pipeline specifically to address the "Goblin incident" — a documented case where a biased reward signal caused a prior model to produce stylistically skewed outputs at scale. That fix matters as much as the headline benchmark numbers. The government-access structure also establishes a new pattern: major US AI model launches may now go through a quiet vetting stage before public release, similar to what happened with Mythos 5. For developers waiting to build on GPT-5.6, "coming weeks" is now the operative timeline — and that delay hands Chinese and open-weight competitors more runway.

### Chinese Open-Weight Models Now Own 61% of Global API Traffic

OpenRouter data confirmed that Chinese open-weight models now account for roughly 61% of all tokens consumed on the platform — the largest neutral LLM router — up from under 2% at the start of 2025. DeepSeek V4 Pro leads the pack at $0.44/$0.87 per million tokens (input/output). US model share has collapsed from 70% to 30% in 12 months. The shift is being driven partly by programming workloads, which now account for more than half of all OpenRouter usage (up from 11% in early 2025); Chinese models have aggressively optimized for code generation benchmarks and price-per-token. A separate story noted that GLM-5.2, from Zhipu AI, dropped its full weights on Hugging Face under an MIT license this weekend — 744B parameters total, 40B active, with a 1 million-token context window.

**Why it matters:** The OpenRouter shift is a demand-side signal that the "advisor model" pattern is going mainstream: route cheap tasks to low-cost open-weight models, escalate only the hardest problems to expensive frontier models. That strategy can cut effective per-token costs by 70–90%, which makes the price differential between US frontier models and Chinese open-weight alternatives practically decisive for any cost-sensitive workload. For OpenAI and Anthropic, this makes the price war being reported by the WSJ not optional — staying at current pricing risks ceding the high-volume agentic workload market to models that cost a tenth as much. For regulators, a world where most API traffic runs through Chinese-originated models raises exactly the questions the Fable 5 export control action was trying to address.

### Goldman Sachs: AI Is Eliminating 11,000 US Jobs Net Per Month

Goldman Sachs' June AI Adoption Tracker puts net AI-driven US job displacement at approximately 11,000 positions per month — lower than the 16,000 figure from its April report, but for a sobering reason: data center construction has been adding roughly 9,000 jobs per month since 2022. Strip out construction, and the displacement in sectors where AI is actually deployed (marketing, customer service, document processing, graphic design, software) looks worse than the headline. Entry-level workers are bearing the brunt: graduate unemployment rose from 3.6% in 2019 to 5.6% in 2026, and 35% of entry-level job postings now require three years of experience. Goldman projects the monthly displacement figure will climb to 22,000–28,000 by Q4 2026 as multimodal AI agents scale from pilots to full deployment.

**Why it matters:** This is one of the most rigorous longitudinal data points on AI's labor market impact available. The construction-sector offset obscures what's actually happening in cognitive work: those jobs are temporary (you build a data center once) while AI automation of knowledge work is structural and compounding. The Gen Z skew is particularly significant — this cohort entered the workforce just as AI coding, writing, and customer service tools became capable enough to close off the entry-level positions that historically provided on-ramp experience. A generation that can't get entry-level jobs because AI does them is a durable political and economic issue, not a short-term adjustment.

## 💻 IT / General Tech News

### Amazon Q Developer: Open a Malicious Repo, Lose Your Cloud Credentials (CVE-2026-12957/58)

Wiz Research publicly disclosed two high-severity vulnerabilities in Amazon Q Developer on June 26 (patched in v1.69.0). The root cause: Amazon Q automatically loaded MCP (Model Context Protocol) server configurations from a `.amazonq/mcp.json` file in any workspace — without asking the user, verifying workspace trust, or sandboxing the process. When a developer opened a malicious Git repository with Amazon Q active, the extension silently executed commands defined in the config. Since spawned processes inherited the developer's full environment, attackers gained immediate access to AWS credentials and any other secrets in the environment. CVE-2026-12957 (CVSS 8.5) covers the trust boundary issue; CVE-2026-12958 covers missing symlink validation. The vulnerabilities were responsibly disclosed April 20, patched May 12, and publicly disclosed June 26.

**Why it matters:** This is the third major AI coding assistant to have a serious MCP-related vulnerability disclosed in 2026, following similar findings in Claude Code and Cursor. The pattern is consistent: MCP's power — allowing AI tools to execute arbitrary local commands — creates a large attack surface when any project file can define what gets executed. Opening a repository and having your cloud credentials silently exfiltrated is a supply-chain attack vector that requires no user interaction beyond git clone. Any developer using Amazon Q should reload their IDE immediately (the language server auto-updates in most configurations). Teams should also audit their security posture around MCP server configs more broadly — this pattern of attack will recur.

### "pedit COW": Linux Kernel 0-Day Grants Root to Unprivileged Users (CVE-2026-46331)

An anonymous GitHub account dropped a fully working exploit for a Linux kernel vulnerability this week, landing at the top of Hacker News with over 900 points. Nicknamed "pedit COW" (a reference to the older Dirty COW exploit), CVE-2026-46331 is an out-of-bounds write in the kernel's traffic-control (`tc pedit`) subsystem that allows any unprivileged local user to gain root on affected systems. The exploit code was published alongside the disclosure with no prior coordination with kernel maintainers. Patches are being expedited through the kernel security process but had not landed in stable kernels as of this writing.

**Why it matters:** Local privilege escalation vulnerabilities of this severity matter most in shared and containerized environments — cloud VMs, Kubernetes nodes, CI/CD runners, and developer sandboxes — where an attacker with any kind of foothold (a compromised container, a malicious package, an unprivileged shell) can immediately escalate to root. The public PoC means the window between disclosure and exploitation in the wild is very short. Linux distributions should expect emergency security updates; sysadmins running shared infrastructure should monitor for patches and apply them immediately. The anonymous, uncoordinated disclosure style is also worth noting as a trend — increasingly common and increasingly dangerous for the patch-before-exploit window.
