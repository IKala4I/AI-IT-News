### Anthropic Accuses Alibaba of the Largest Known Distillation Attack on Any AI Lab

Anthropic sent a letter to the U.S. Senate Committee on Banking, Housing, and Urban Affairs accusing Alibaba of running the largest known distillation attack against any AI company to date. Between April 22 and June 5, operators linked to Alibaba's Qwen AI lab conducted 28.8 million exchanges with Claude models using roughly 25,000 fraudulent accounts. The technique — called "adversarial distillation" — involves systematically prompting a frontier model to harvest its reasoning patterns and response structures, then using those outputs to train a competing internal model. The attack targeted Claude's most valuable capabilities: software engineering and agentic reasoning. Anthropic has notified the White House and says Alibaba "ignored the Trump Administration's warnings" about the practice.

**Why it matters:** This is the clearest public accusation yet of a systematic, large-scale effort by one major AI lab to extract the capabilities of a rival by weaponizing its own API against it. Distillation attacks sit in a legal grey zone — they don't involve hacking or credential theft, just API calls — but Anthropic is explicitly framing it as IP theft and escalating it to the Senate and the executive branch. If the accusation holds up, it adds a new dimension to the US–China AI competition: not just export controls on chips and models, but now enforcement of inference API terms of service as a national security matter.

### Claude Tag: Anthropic Puts a Persistent AI Agent Inside Your Slack

Anthropic launched Claude Tag in beta on June 23 for Enterprise and Team subscribers. Unlike a standard chatbot integration, Claude Tag gives teams a shared AI identity in Slack — any employee can @-mention it, hand off half-finished work, and have it operate asynchronously across channels. It breaks complex tasks into stages, runs them independently, and reports back in-thread. An "ambient" mode lets it proactively flag relevant updates across the organization without being asked. Internally, Anthropic says Claude Tag is already approving and incorporating 65% of the code changes submitted by its own product team.

**Why it matters:** Most AI integrations in enterprise chat tools behave like a smarter search bar — you ask, it answers, session ends. Claude Tag is a different model: it has persistent organizational memory, can hold state across asynchronous handoffs, and operates without requiring a human to wait in the loop. The 65% internal code-merge figure is notable not as a benchmark but as a usage signal — Anthropic is itself running a significant fraction of its software development through the system it's selling. For enterprise customers, this is the most concrete Anthropic deployment story since Claude for Work.

### OpenAI's "Patch the Planet" Puts GPT-5.5-Cyber to Work on Open-Source Security

On June 22, OpenAI launched "Patch the Planet" as part of its Daybreak cybersecurity initiative, built with Trail of Bits, HackerOne, and Calif. The program is scanning widely-used open-source projects for vulnerabilities using GPT-5.5-Cyber combined with human security engineer review. More than 30 projects have joined, including cURL, Go, Python, Sigstore, and pyca/cryptography. In the first week, the system analyzed over 30 million lines of code, generated 8 kernel pointer information-leak proof-of-concepts, produced 24 local privilege escalation exploits, and identified 5 exploitable vulnerabilities in Chrome's V8 JavaScript engine — 3 of which were reported and fixed within days of being introduced.

**Why it matters:** The gap between "AI finds security bugs" demos and "AI running in production security workflows at scale" has been large. Patch the Planet is the most credible large-scale attempt yet to deploy AI-assisted vulnerability discovery in an end-to-end, human-supervised pipeline at open-source infrastructure scale. The Chrome V8 findings are particularly striking: identifying and remediating exploitable vulnerabilities within days of introduction is significantly faster than any current manual process. If this scales, it has implications for the mean-time-to-patch metric across all software, not just the enrolled projects.

### Meta Is Building a Prediction Market App — With AI Resolving the Bets

Internal documents reported by the New York Times and NPR reveal that Meta CEO Mark Zuckerberg has directed a team to build a standalone prediction market app codenamed "Arena" (also called "Antwerp" / "FBForecast" in earlier documents). The app will use Llama to automatically generate questions from trending topics, make personalized market recommendations to users, and — notably — use AI to resolve markets in near-real-time. Users bet with a "daily virtual allotment" of play money rather than real currency, putting it outside gambling regulations. A prototype will be tested internally before a public iOS and Android launch; no date has been specified.

**Why it matters:** Prediction markets have been validated as a genuinely useful forecasting mechanism (Polymarket, Kalshi), but they're currently niche and friction-heavy. Meta has the distribution — billions of users, app store presence, and an engagement flywheel — to bring this to mainstream scale. Letting an LLM resolve markets automatically is the most consequential design decision in the product: it's cheap, fast, and scalable, but it also introduces a new kind of manipulation surface (prompt-jacking the resolution model). The competitive knock-on is significant: Kalshi was reportedly raising at a $40B valuation; Meta entering for free with Llama resolution changes the calculus for the whole sector.

### Ford Rehires 350 Engineers After AI Quality Inspection Falls Short

Bloomberg reported today that Ford has quietly hired 350 veteran engineers — many of them retired former employees — over the past three years to address quality problems its AI tools were unable to solve. Ford brought back what it calls "gray beard" engineers to train younger staff and to reprogram the AI inspection systems that were missing failure patterns that experienced humans could spot. The approach is working: Ford topped the JD Power Initial Quality Survey this week as the best mainstream automotive brand — its best result in years.

**Why it matters:** This is a counterweight to the dominant narrative that AI is replacing all human expertise in manufacturing. Ford tried to let AI handle quality inspection at scale, found it couldn't preserve or transfer the tacit knowledge that veteran engineers carry, and had to bring those humans back — not just to check the AI's work, but to improve it. The lesson is about training data quality, not AI capability: the AI systems degraded because the human expertise that their training data depended on had been retired. This pattern — AI performing well on known failure modes, humans needed to recognize novel ones — will likely repeat across other industries as headcount cuts remove the knowledge generation the AI ultimately relies on.

### OpenRouter Fusion: Combine Three Mid-Tier Models, Beat a Frontier at Half the Cost

A configuration for OpenRouter's new "Fusion" feature has been making rounds in AI developer communities (surfaced by the @ai_for_devs Telegram channel). Fusion runs multiple models in parallel and synthesizes their outputs using a judge model. Community testing showed that combining Gemini 3 Flash + Kimi K2.6 + DeepSeek V4 Pro through Fusion matched Fable 5's output quality on a range of tasks while outperforming GPT-5.5 and Opus 4.8 individually — at roughly half the cost of running Fable 5 directly. The configuration is accessible to any OpenRouter user with an API key.

**Why it matters:** The Fable 5 and Mythos 5 shutdown exposed the risk of single-model dependency in production AI workflows. Fusion-style architectures — running cheap, open models in parallel with a synthesis layer — are the infrastructure answer: more resilient, cheaper per token, and in this case apparently competitive on quality. The practical implication for developers: routing budget toward clever ensemble configurations may now yield better cost-quality tradeoffs than spending up for frontier models, particularly for tasks that benefit from diverse reasoning paths rather than raw capability depth.
