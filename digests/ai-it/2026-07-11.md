### GPT-5.6 Goes Fully Public — Three-Tier Sol/Terra/Luna Family, SOTA Coding Claims, and a Quiet Government Review

Yesterday's digest noted OpenAI had made GPT-5.6 Microsoft's "preferred model." Today the full picture became clear: GPT-5.6 is actually three models — Sol (flagship, aimed at hard coding and security work), Terra (balanced everyday model), and Luna (fast/cheap) — priced at $5/$30, $2.50/$15, and $1/$6 per million input/output tokens respectively. The family had been quietly restricted to ~20 US-government-vetted organizations since June 26 before opening to apps and the API on July 9-10. OpenAI claims Sol hit 80.0 on the Artificial Analysis Coding Agent Index (2.8 points above Claude Opus 4.8) while using under half the tokens of comparable runs; a new "ultra" setting runs four agents in parallel on hard problems. In a July 9 CNBC interview, Sam Altman confirmed OpenAI made "many changes" to the model during pre-launch talks with Commerce Secretary Lutnick and Treasury Secretary Bessent.

**Why it matters:** Beyond the benchmark race, this is the first confirmed instance of a US administration reviewing a frontier model before public release — a precedent worth watching regardless of how the Sol/Opus benchmark dispute shakes out.

### OpenAI Says GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra Cracked a 47-Year-Old Math Conjecture — Mathematicians Are Still Checking

OpenAI published a write-up claiming Sol Ultra produced a proof of the Cycle Double Cover Conjecture, a graph-theory problem posed by Paul Seymour in 1979, in under an hour by running 64 parallel subagents on competing approaches with cross-auditing between them. No formal (Lean) verification has been done yet — the proof is currently being checked by working mathematicians — and it was the single most-discussed story of the past two days, topping Hacker News with nearly 500 points and over 400 comments.

**Why it matters:** If the proof holds up under peer scrutiny it would be a genuine landmark for AI-assisted mathematics; if it doesn't, it's a cautionary tale about taking "solved" claims from a model's own vendor at face value before independent verification.

Sources: [OpenAI proof PDF](https://cdn.openai.com/pdf/cdc_proof.pdf) · [Hacker News discussion](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48863490)

### Mistral Enters Robotics With a Camera-Only Navigation Model

Mistral released Robostral Navigate, an 8B-parameter model that lets robots navigate complex environments using a single ordinary RGB camera — no depth sensors — trained entirely in simulation. It scored 76.6% on the R2R-CE navigation benchmark, beating the best single-camera method by 9.7 points and even beating multi-camera/depth-sensor systems by 4.5 points, and is hardware-agnostic enough to run on any robot fleet.

**Why it matters:** It's Mistral's first move into "physical AI," joining NVIDIA, Hugging Face, and Meta in a broader industry push to get AI out of chat windows and into robots this quarter.

Sources: [Mistral AI](https://mistral.ai/news/robostral-navigate/) · [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-08/mistral-ai-releases-robotics-model-to-support-physical-ai-push)

### Apple Sues OpenAI, Alleging Its Former iPhone Design Chief Stole Trade Secrets for AI Hardware

Apple filed a federal lawsuit against OpenAI and two former Apple employees: Tang Tan, Apple's former VP of iPhone/Apple Watch product design and now OpenAI's hardware chief, and Chang Liu, a former senior systems electrical engineer. Apple alleges the theft happened "at every level" — that Tan directed Apple staff interviewing at OpenAI to share confidential information, and that Liu kept an Apple laptop after leaving and used it to download technical documents. Apple is seeking damages and an injunction. The two companies partnered on Apple Intelligence/ChatGPT integration as recently as 2024.

**Why it matters:** It turns the AI hardware race — OpenAI's long-rumored consumer device — into an open legal fight between two companies that were recently partners, and raises the stakes for anyone hiring across the Apple/OpenAI talent pipeline.

### Meta Greenlights September Production of Its In-House "Iris" AI Chip

An internal Meta memo reviewed by Reuters shows the company will begin manufacturing its custom "Iris" data-center AI chip in September — part of a four-generation MTIA silicon roadmap co-designed with Broadcom and fabricated by TSMC. The goal is to double Meta's computing capacity to 14 gigawatts next year and cut its dependence on Nvidia and AMD; six weeks of internal testing reportedly found no major issues, a turnaround after years of a troubled in-house chip effort.

**Why it matters:** A working custom chip at this scale would meaningfully reduce Meta's exposure to Nvidia pricing and supply constraints, following the same playbook Google and Amazon have used with their own AI silicon.

### Cambridge Study: Boko Haram Has Deeply Embedded Frontier AI Chatbots Into Its Operations

A University of Cambridge Frontier AI Working Paper by researcher Antonia Juelich, based on roughly 60 interviews with 27 former Boko Haram members, found the group uses ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Grok, and DeepSeek — deliberately mixing platforms to dodge safety guardrails — for weapons design and upgrades, tactical planning, operational security, and after-action reviews, with dedicated "AI units" advising commanders behind the front line. One documented case: AI helped the group figure out how to modify motorcycles to clear defensive trenches after an attack was initially repelled.

**Why it matters:** It's one of the most concrete field documentations yet of frontier chatbots being operationally embedded in an active armed group, not just theoretical misuse — it was the #2 story on Hacker News with over 200 points and widely picked up by Nigerian press.

### NYC Becomes First US City to Mandate "Click-to-Cancel" for Subscriptions

Mayor Mamdani's administration finalized a rule, effective October 1, requiring any business that lets New Yorkers subscribe online to also let them cancel online — banning dark patterns like forced phone calls, hidden cancellation flows, or misrepresented costs. Penalties start at $525 per violation, rising to $3,500 for repeat offenders; banks, credit unions, and other state-regulated financial entities are exempt.

**Why it matters:** It's a concrete, enforceable local template other cities and states are likely to copy, and it's a direct compliance requirement for any IT/product team running subscription billing that serves NYC customers — it was Hacker News's #1 story with nearly 600 points.
