## 🤖 AI Models & Research

### Teleoperated Humanoid Robots Perform Live Surgery for the First Time

A University of California San Diego team published results in *Nature* showing humanoid robots — nicknamed "Surgie" and built on the Unitree G1 platform — successfully performed two gallbladder-removal surgeries on live pigs. In one, a robot worked alongside a human surgeon acting as assistant; in the other, two robots operated together with no human at the table, all movements remotely controlled by skilled human surgeons. The robots are notably smaller and less space-intensive than existing surgical robot systems used in hospitals today.

**Why it matters:** It's a genuine world-first for humanoid (rather than purpose-built) surgical robots, and a concrete data point for how general-purpose robot bodies plus remote human control could extend specialist surgical skill to locations that can't afford or staff a dedicated surgical robot.

### AI Safety Researchers Publish "AI 2040: Plan A" — A Blueprint for Avoiding an AI Arms Race

The AI Futures Project — the group behind the widely-discussed "AI 2027" forecast — published a new scenario called "Plan A," proposing that the US and China deliberately delay development of superintelligence until 2040, make frontier AI research public, let a broader set of companies globally catch up to the cutting edge, and enter a mutual-deterrence regime around compute buildouts. The authors frame it explicitly as a policy vehicle, not a prediction, and contrast it with four alternative paths (B, C, D, S) for how the US could respond to superintelligence. It became the top story on Hacker News and drew coverage from The Washington Post and Axios.

**Why it matters:** It's one of the more detailed public attempts yet at a concrete, cooperative alternative to the current AI race dynamic — worth watching whether it gains any traction with policymakers versus simply sparking online debate.

## 🏢 AI Industry & Funding

### OpenAI's Safety Head Departs — Sixth Safety Leader to Exit in Two Years

Johannes Heidecke, who has led OpenAI's safety systems team since 2024, told colleagues he's leaving the company by July 24. As part of the reorganization, OpenAI is folding its safety function into its research organization under VP of Research Mia Glaese, eliminating safety's previously independent standing; Saachi Jain becomes interim head of safety systems in the meantime. Heidecke joins a list of departed safety-focused leaders stretching back to 2024, including Jan Leike, Miles Brundage, Steven Adler, Andrea Vallone, and former Chief Futurist Joshua Achiam.

**Why it matters:** A sixth senior safety exit in two years, combined with the removal of safety's independent org structure, is a pattern worth tracking regardless of OpenAI's stated rationale — it shapes how much internal pushback capacity remains inside the company as it races toward an IPO and ever-more-capable models.

### Microsoft's Emissions Jump 25% as AI Datacenter Buildout Accelerates

Microsoft's 2026 Annual Sustainability Report shows the company's carbon emissions reached 20 million metric tons of CO2-equivalent, up 25% from 16 million tons in 2024. The biggest driver was a spike in Scope 2 emissions (from purchased electricity) — now 13% of Microsoft's total footprint, up from just 2% the year before — caused by new datacenter construction and Microsoft's decision to pause purchases of certain renewable-energy credits.

**Why it matters:** Microsoft pledged in 2020 to be carbon-negative by 2030; this report is a fairly stark admission that its AI infrastructure buildout is actively working against that goal, and it's a preview of the environmental accounting reckoning likely coming for every other hyperscaler running similar datacenter expansions.

### Scrutiny Grows Over "Circular Financing" Between Nvidia, CoreWeave, and Nebius

A widely-discussed financial analysis (a top Hacker News story this week) lays out how neocloud AI infrastructure providers CoreWeave and Nebius are burning cash far faster than they earn it — CoreWeave's capex ran $7.7B in a recent quarter against just $2.08B in revenue, producing negative $4.71B free cash flow, with debt at $24.86B and interest payments eating up over a quarter of revenue. Nvidia both invests in these companies and is their primary GPU supplier, sometimes backstopping unsold capacity, creating a loop where Nvidia effectively finances customers to buy its own chips.

**Why it matters:** This circular-financing structure has been flagged before, but the underlying numbers keep getting worse, not better — it's a concrete illustration of the leverage propping up the "AI infrastructure boom" narrative, and a scenario worth watching if capital markets get pickier about neocloud debt.

## 💻 IT / General Tech News

### "HalluSquatting" Attack Turns AI Coding Assistants' Hallucinations Into a Botnet Vector

Security researchers disclosed a new attack technique called HalluSquatting, which exploits the tendency of LLM coding assistants (including GitHub Copilot, Gemini CLI, and others) to hallucinate plausible-but-fake package names, repo URLs, or tool identifiers. Attackers use LLMs themselves to predict which hallucinated names are likely to come up, register those names in advance, and plant malicious payloads there — so when a coding agent "helpfully" fetches the resource it made up, it downloads and runs attacker-controlled code. Researchers found hallucination rates as high as 85% in repository-cloning tasks and up to 100% in some "skill installation" scenarios, enough to potentially assemble large botnets or run scalable ransomware campaigns.

**Why it matters:** Unlike classic typosquatting, this attack targets a model's own reasoning process rather than user typos, making it much harder for a user to notice or avoid — and it affects essentially every popular AI coding tool, not a single vulnerable product.

Sources: [The Hacker News](https://thehackernews.com/2026/07/new-hallusquatting-attack-could-trick.html)

## 🛠️ New Tools & Releases

### NVIDIA Ships Nemotron 3 Ultra, a 550B-Parameter Open-Weights Agentic Model

NVIDIA released Nemotron 3 Ultra, its largest open-weights model to date at 550B total parameters (55B active, mixture-of-experts), tuned specifically for agentic workloads and combining strong benchmark intelligence with fast output (300+ tokens/second). Alongside the model, LangChain and NVIDIA launched "NemoClaw," a Deep Agents blueprint that pairs Nemotron 3 Ultra with LangChain's Deep Agents framework and NVIDIA's OpenShell runtime so enterprises can build and self-host governed agent systems rather than renting one from a closed API.

**Why it matters:** It's a meaningful open-weights alternative for enterprises wary of full dependence on closed frontier APIs, and reflects a broader trend (also voiced this week by Hugging Face's CEO) of companies moving from "renting" AI to running and owning tuned open models as usage scales up.

Sources: [NVIDIA Newsroom](https://nvidianews.nvidia.com/news/nvidia-debuts-nemotron-3-family-of-open-models) · [LangChain](https://www.langchain.com/blog/langchain-and-nvidia-launch-the-nemoclaw-deep-agents-blueprint)
